# Why the matrix + pomodoro combo works

Productivity apps usually pick a side.

**Todo apps** (Todoist, Things, TickTick) are great at capturing and listing tasks. They're bad at deciding which one to do *now*, and they don't track whether you actually worked on it.

**Pomodoro apps** (Forest, Be Focused) are great at timing focus blocks. They don't know anything about your tasks, your priorities, or your week.

**Project tools** (Jira, Linear, Asana) are great at team visibility. They bury your personal planning under process overhead.

Zen stitches two small ideas together:

1. **The Eisenhower Matrix** decides *what*. Urgent+Important goes in TODO. Important-not-urgent goes in SCHEDULE. The rest gets deprioritized or dropped.
2. **The Pomodoro timer** decides *when*. Pick a task from TODO, start 25 minutes, go.

The payoff: every pomodoro is attached to a task, every task has a priority, and at end of day you have a standup that writes itself from what you actually worked on.

## Who this is for

- Developers juggling 2–4 concurrent clients who need to justify their hours
- Remote ICs who want their weekly standup ready before the meeting
- Solo founders fighting shiny-object syndrome

## Who this isn't for

- Team leads tracking other people's work (use Sunday or Linear)
- Non-developers wanting a generic todo (use Todoist)
- Agencies needing shared boards (use Sunday, Jira, Asana)

## What I'm trying to avoid

Feature creep. The temptation with a productivity tool is to add tags, filters, automations, AI, until it becomes a mini-OS. I'm trying to keep Zen narrow: **the developer's personal workflow, and nothing else**.
